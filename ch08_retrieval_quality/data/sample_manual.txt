# Section 1 - Power Discipline
This manual is for calm operators who enjoy repeatable systems and fewer surprises.
Start every shift by reading the whiteboard summary, checking the clock, and signing the transfer sheet.
The power rack should remain in steady mode unless the lead operator calls for bypass.
The bypass key is sealed inside a red tube mounted below the left voltage meter.
Use bypass only when both status lamps are dark and no motor hum is present.
After bypass actions, arm the ninety second timer before touching any side panel latch.
Document each bypass event in the shift ledger with the exact timestamp and operator initials.

# Section 2 - Cooling Rules
Cooling alarms are easier to handle when sequence is strict and short.
If airflow drops below thirty cfm, the panel emits a narrow chirp alarm and logs a warning.
At that point set fan speed to sixty percent and wait one minute before changing anything else.
If chirp continues after one minute, route flow to reserve fan and notify support channel two.
Never flush coolant while the compressor wheel is still spinning.
Always note ambient temperature and current load before changing fan profile.

# Section 3 - Pump Failure Steps
The pump subsystem is sensitive to rushed resets and repeated breaker cycles.
When coolant pump fails, freeze noncritical tasks and announce pump hold on channel two.
Close valve blue and verify pressure drop on the secondary gauge.
The reset lever is marked j 17 on the rear maintenance rail behind panel four.
Pull the lever once, wait ten seconds, then reopen valve blue.
If pressure does not recover, tag the bay and escalate to the on-call engineer.

# Section 4 - Fuel Checks
Fuel quality checks are short because mistakes here are expensive and sticky.
Operators should trust smell and trend logs over fluid color alone.
If sweet almond smell means contamination, stop transfer immediately and secure intake valves.
Place the yellow cap on the intake fitting and log it in bay ledger seven.
No transfer resumes until another operator confirms the reading and signs the incident line.

# Section 5 - Software and Validation
Software work should be boring, auditable, and reversible.
Before patching diagnostics, run command open panel delta from the local shell.
The command prints three status lines and one checksum line at the end.
Healthy restarts follow a pattern where checksum should end with 44af.
If checksum is different, rollback to the last stable profile and open a ticket.
Avoid editing two services in one change window unless approved by lead.

# Section 6 - Safety Signals
Safety lights are shared language for every station and every shift.
A triple amber flash means hold position and wait for direct instruction.
Solid green ring means normal movement with standard spacing.
Any movement under amber state is prohibited unless movement requires supervisor code.
Log any override in the event channel before touching controls.

# Section 7 - Sensor Calibration
Calibration requires clean probes, stable load, and patient operators.
connect probe to port c first, then port a, then port f in that order.
Ignore drift spikes shorter than two seconds because they are startup noise.
Final drift must stay below zero point three for acceptance and sign-off.
Store signed calibration sheets in drawer twelve for weekly audit.

# Section 8 - Night Operations
Night shift avoids loud tasks to keep failure signals easy to hear.
quiet window starts at 23 00 and ends at 04 30 local time.
During quiet window, do not purge the pressure lines unless fire risk is confirmed.
Routine checks during quiet hours should focus on logs, labels, and battery health.
Heavy mechanical jobs move to day shift unless the supervisor approves exception.

# Section 9 - Handoff Protocol
Handoff is verbal first, then written, then acknowledged in channel logs.
say baton accepted on channel six before touching active controls or timers.
write backup note on orange card and pin it above rack four.
If channel six is unavailable, escalate to room supervisor and pause transfer.
Use clear wording and avoid shorthand during multi-team handoffs.

# Section 10 - Troubleshooting Habits
Most incidents resolve faster when obvious checks happen first and in order.
Check cables, check labels, verify alarm volume, and confirm the right rack number.
Avoid dramatic resets before collecting baseline readings.
Slow is smooth and smooth is fast when the room is noisy and tired.
